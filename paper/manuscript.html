Abstract

We examine the use of the probability of direction (pd) as a decision rule in psychological research, where results are often interpreted using conventional thresholds (e.g., pd > 0.975). When such thresholds are applied repeatedly across multiple parameters, a multiplicity problem arises, as the probability of obtaining at least one misleading conclusion increases with the number of tests. Here, we characterize the operating properties of pd-based decisions under repeated use, exploiting its formal correspondence with the frequentist p-value in common settings. Through simulation, we show that extreme pd values can arise by chance, leading to inflated rates of misleading evidence. To address this issue, we recast pd in terms of posterior odds and derive a prior-odds adjustment that accounts for multiplicity by down-weighting the prior probability assigned to each individual hypothesis. This approach separates within-model priors from prior assumptions over the hypothesis space, allowing researchers to control error rates while maintaining flexibility in within-model priors specification. The method is extended to account for correlations among tested hypotheses and is benchmarked against standard frequentist corrections. An accompanying R package implements the proposed procedure, facilitating its use in applied research.

Keywords: probability of direction, multiple testing, prior-odds


Adjusting the Probability of Direction for Multiple Testing

Over the past two decades, the use of Bayesian methods in psychological research has grown considerably (Jevremov & Pajić, 2024; Pfadt et al., 2025; Van De Schoot et al., 2017). This expansion has, however, been accompanied by practices reminiscent of the “null ritual” in frequentist statistics, where widespread adoption gave way to automatic application rather than principled reasoning (Cohen, 1994; Gigerenzer, 2004). For instance, researchers sometimes treat Bayes Factor thresholds (e.g., BF > 3) as dichotomous decision rules, or claim that an effect “exists” because the posterior probability of a directional effect exceeds a conventional benchmark such as 95% (Heck et al., 2023; Hoijtink et al., 2016; Kelter, 2023; Kruschke, 2018; Tendeiro et al., 2024; Tendeiro & Kiers, 2019). While such summaries are convenient, their use as default cutoffs encourages all-or-none interpretations. When these binary judgments are applied repeatedly across many parameters, they generate a multiplicity problem no different in kind from the one long recognized in frequentist statistics.

Yet even there, the problem of multiplicity has been handled inconsistently in practice. Researchers typically account for multiplicity in well-recognized cases, such as post hoc comparisons, but rarely do so when evaluating several effects simultaneously within the same model (Zeevi et al., 2025). This is routine in ANOVA or multiple regression, where main effects, interactions, and multiple predictors each constitute a distinct hypothesis test. Either way, the probability of at least one false positive accumulates with the number of tests performed, regardless of how those tests are framed or grouped (Bender & Lange, 2001; Cramer et al., 2016).

Frequentist methods have been developed to address precisely this accumulation, targeting either the family-wise error rate (the probability of at least one false positive) or the false discovery rate (the expected proportion of false positives among rejected hypotheses) (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995; Westfall & Young, 1993). These procedures address whether a spurious result enters the literature, but leave unresolved a subtler consequence of the same selection process. When researchers examine many parameters but highlight only the most extreme estimates, selection systematically inflates reported magnitudes (Altoè et al., 2020; Gelman & Carlin, 2014; Zwet & Cator, 2021).

Bayesian methods can mitigate this overestimation by pulling extreme estimates toward more plausible values, via informative priors (Dawid, 1994; Zwet & Gelman, 2022) and hierarchical modelling (Berry & Hochberg, 1999; Gelman et al., 2012, 2013; Kruschke, 2011). Crucially, however, magnitude bias and false positive accumulation are related but distinct problems. Shrinkage does not, on its own, provide any guarantee about the error properties of a repeated decision rule applied across many parameters: if the same cutoff is applied to many parameters (e.g., declaring an effect whenever the posterior probability exceeds 95%), the probability of at least one spurious finding can remain substantial. Bayesian solutions to this issue typically rely on either simultaneous credible statements (Box & Tiao, 1992; Gelman & Tuerlinckx, 2000; Pruzek, 2016; Schnell et al., 2020) or priors over the hypothesis space that scale with the number of tests (Jeffreys, 1938; Scott & Berger, 2006; Scott & Berger, 2010; Westfall et al., 1997). Despite these available tools, hierarchical modeling alone is still routinely treated as sufficient for multiplicity control, a conflation that leaves the problem only partially addressed (Chen et al., 2020; Erčulj & Štrumbelj, 2015; Hogeveen et al., 2022; Limbachia et al., 2021; Mathar et al., 2022).

At this point, one might object that multiplicity concerns do not arise under a subjectivist interpretation of Bayesian inference. This view treats posterior probabilities as rational degrees of personal belief, and therefore not subject to frequentist error criteria (Edwards et al., 1963; Lindley, 1972; Rozeboom, 1960). It requires, however, both a coherent prior specification and coherent joint inference over all parameters of interest. These conditions are rarely satisfied in applied psychological research, where default weakly informative or uninformative priors are the norm (Smid & Winter, 2020; Van De Schoot et al., 2017). Moreover, when such priors are used, or when samples are sufficiently large, Bayesian and frequentist procedures often converge (Albers et al., 2018; Bernardo & Smith, 1994; Gelman et al., 2013), implying that multiplicity concerns cannot be dismissed simply by adopting a Bayesian approach.

Rather than adjudicating these positions further, here we adopt a falsificationist approach (Box, 1980; Gelman & Hennig, 2017; Gelman & Shalizi, 2013; Mayo, 2018) and ask a more tractable question: what are the operating characteristics of a widely used Bayesian decision rule, and how can its multiplicity problem be addressed in a principled way?

We focus on the Probability of Direction (pd) (Makowski et al., 2019), a posterior index that quantifies the probability that an effect falls in its dominant direction and whose use in applied psychological research has grown substantially in recent years (Ben-Artzi et al., 2022; Bramley & Ruggeri, 2022; Brissenden et al., 2023, 2024; Ciranka et al., 2026; Hochman et al., 2025; Makowski et al., 2020; Schurr et al., 2024; Stone-Sabali et al., 2024). A key property of pd is that it can be formally linked to the frequentist p-value in standard settings (Berger & Sellke, 1987; Casella & Berger, 1987; Marsman & Wagenmakers, 2017). This correspondence makes the error consequences of thresholding pd, and therefore the multiplicity problem it generates, directly interpretable in terms familiar to most researchers.

The paper is organized as follows. We first introduce pd and examine its use as a test. We then characterize the multiplicity problem that arises under repeated thresholding, discussing the role of prior choice and explaining why skeptical priors alone are insufficient to resolve it. Next, we develop the prior-odds adjustment for multiplicity control, extending the framework of Jeffreys (1938) and Westfall et al. (1997) to pd: we move from posterior probabilities to posterior odds, derive the adjustment procedure, discuss how the prior odds should be chosen, and evaluate the approach via simulation. We then extend the framework to account for correlation among tests, and we conclude with a worked example using the accompanying R package (Calderan, 2026), followed by a general discussion.

Probability of Direction

The probability of direction (pd) is a posterior index that quantifies how strongly the posterior distribution favors one direction of an effect relative to a reference (\theta_{null}) value (Makowski et al., 2019). For a continuous posterior, it can be defined as the larger of the two one-sided posterior probabilities: pd = \max(\Pr(\theta < \theta_{null} \mid y),\ \Pr(\theta > \theta_{null} \mid y)), and it ranges from 0.50 (no directional preference) to 1 (all on one side). Because pd is derived from the posterior, it depends on the full model specification (likelihood and prior), so the long-run error behavior of pd-based decisions is not a property of pd “in isolation” but of the overall Bayesian procedure that generates the posterior.

A convenient starting point for calibration is the mapping between pd and the frequentist p-value. Under flat (or sufficiently diffuse symmetric) priors in typical hypothesis-testing scenarios (e.g., testing means), pd is the posterior probability of the data-favored sign relative to \theta_{null}. This value corresponds to the complement of the one-sided p-value (Casella & Berger, 1987; Marsman & Wagenmakers, 2017), yielding the two-sided approximation p \approx 2(1 - pd). In practice, the pd is often used to summarize both the direction and existence of an effect.

This interpretation is clearest when phrased as a decision rule pd>c: the posterior must assign more than c probability mass to the side of \theta_{\text{null}} favored by the data. Setting c=1-\alpha/2 is convenient because it bounds the posterior mass in the opposite tail by \alpha/2. For concreteness, suppose the posterior favors a positive effect so that pd=\Pr(\theta>0\mid y). An \alpha=0.05 implies c=0.975, therefore the rule pd>c requires that \Pr(\theta\le \theta_{\text{null}}\mid y)<0.025, placing less than 2.5% of posterior mass at or below 0. As Figure 1 illustrates, observing pd>0.975 is equivalent to excluding \theta_{\text{null}} from the central 95% equi-tailed credible interval. When the credible interval excludes the null value, one can infer that the null is relatively implausible (Box & Tiao, 1992).

More formally, let q_p denote the p-th posterior quantile, so the central (1-\alpha) equi-tailed credible interval is [q_{\alpha/2},\,q_{1-\alpha/2}], which leaves probability \alpha/2 in each tail. The condition \Pr(\theta\le \theta_{\text{null}}\mid y)<\alpha/2 holds if and only if \theta_{\text{null}} falls below q_{\alpha/2}, that is, when \theta_{\text{null}}\notin [q_{\alpha/2},\,q_{1-\alpha/2}].

Given these considerations, although pd and the frequentist p-value target different quantities, it is expected that decision rules of the form pd>c approximate the operating characteristics of familiar frequentist rules such as p<\alpha.

Although we set \theta_{\text{null}} = 0 throughout for ease of exposition, the procedure extends readily to non-zero nulls. This is relevant, for example, when hypotheses concern a minimum effect of practical interest (Lakens et al., 2018). In such cases, the direction of the effect must be specified explicitly, that is, whether the parameter is expected to exceed or fall below \theta_{\text{null}}, and the pd is computed as the posterior probability in that declared direction rather than taking the maximum over both sides.

Multiplicity Problem

Prior impact

Under the null hypothesis, p-values are uniformly distributed on [0, 1]. Because pd can be mapped to the p-value, this implies a corresponding null reference behavior for pd on [0.5, 1]. However, because pd is a posterior probability regarding the sign of \theta, its null distribution is not determined by the likelihood alone: it can shift depending on how strongly the prior concentrates probability near 0 (i.e., how strongly the prior “expects” near-null effects). Demonstrating this prior dependence at m = 1 makes the target of subsequent multiplicity analyses explicit. Even before any multiplicity is introduced, a fixed cutoff, such as pd > 0.975, does not have a universal consequence across priors and sample sizes.

Given these considerations, as a first step, we simulated data under the null (\theta = 0) and computed the frequency of pd > c, with c = 0.975 (\alpha = 0.05). Operationally, this quantifies how often the procedure falsely excludes zero from the 95% central credible interval (bounded by 1-c and c), incorrectly concluding that a directed effect exists[^1]. For the frequentist comparison, we conducted two-sided, one-sample z-tests (with \alpha = 0.05). Specifically, we conducted a Monte Carlo simulation with 10,000 replications for sample sizes n \in \{30, 50, 100\}. In each replication, data were drawn from a standard normal distribution, y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1). For every simulated dataset, we computed the posterior distribution of \theta using a conjugate normal-normal model with y_i \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1) and prior \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2). We varied the prior scale \tau \in \{0.2, 0.5, 1, 1.5\} to encompass a range of priors, from skeptical to relatively diffuse.

As shown in Figure 2, pd behaves analogously to a p-value when samples are large and the prior is diffuse (\tau \geq 1): the null distribution is approximately uniform on [0.5, 1]. Under skeptical priors (\tau = 0.2), however, the distribution concentrates away from the extremes, and the observed error rate drops.

Why skeptical priors alone are insufficient

Building on this baseline, we examine the effects of multiplicity. Although individual tests rarely produce an extreme pd, the probability of observing at least one extreme value among pd_1, \dots, pd_m increases with the number of tests, m. We quantify this behavior by extending the simulation to families of size m \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10, 20\} and computing the proportion of replications in which at least one pd > 0.975. This quantity captures the expected rate of misleading evidence as a function of m; for readability, we refer to it as the family-wise error rate (FWER).

As expected, pd-based decisions behave broadly analogously to their frequentist counterparts when sample sizes are large and the prior places relatively little mass near 0 (Figure 3). By contrast, as the prior becomes more skeptical (i.e., increasingly concentrated around 0), the FWER decreases. Nevertheless, the larger the sample, the less the impact of prior.

The simulation results suggest that error rates can be reduced by adopting more skeptical within-model priors for each parameter. However, relying on prior skepticism as a general remedy for multiplicity conflates two distinct goals: (i) encoding substantive prior knowledge about a specific parameter, and (ii) calibrating the error rate of a decision rule under repeated testing. When the primary aim is error-rate control, adjusting the within-model prior introduces bias into parameter estimates as an unintended side effect.

The approach presented below instead handles multiplicity at the level of prior odds, while leaving within-model priors unchanged. In particular, researchers can explicitly encode their expectations about how many effects are likely to be present, allowing multiplicity to be addressed, but without altering the underlying model (or models). This separation allows researchers to select the most appropriate prior for each parameter based on substantive knowledge, while still controlling error rates when making multiple statements.

One condition, however, must be met: becausepd measures the probability mass on one side of the null\theta_{\text{null}}, the within-model prior must assign equal plausibility to effects in either direction relative to \theta_{\text{null}}. This requirement is satisfied by all standard default priors, and still leaves the choice of prior informativeness entirely at the researcher’s discretion.

Prior-Odds adjustment for multiplicity

From posterior probabilities to posterior odds

To connect pd to posterior odds, consider the directional hypotheses H_{+}: \theta > 0 and H_{-}: \theta < 0. These hypotheses partition the parameter space based on the sign of \theta. If the prior for \theta is continuous and symmetric about 0, then the two directions are equally plausible a priori: \Pr(H_{+}) = \Pr(H_{-}) = 0.5.

Without loss of generality, assume the posterior favors H_{+}, such that pd = \Pr(H_{+} \mid y) = \Pr(\theta > 0 \mid y). The complement is then given by 1 - pd = \Pr(\theta \le 0 \mid y). The posterior odds comparing these two hypotheses are therefore: \frac{pd}{1 - pd} = \frac{\Pr(H_{+} \mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-0} \mid y)}

For a continuous posterior distribution, the probability of any single point (e.g., \Pr(\theta = 0 \mid y)) is 0, which means that one could write \frac{pd}{1 - pd} = \frac{\Pr(H_{+} \mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-} \mid y)}. However, we retain the notation H_{-0} in the denominator to explicitly indicate that the hypothesis includes the point null value of zero.

Applying Bayes’ rule, these posterior odds can be decomposed into a Bayes Factor and prior odds: \frac{\Pr(H_{+} \mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-0} \mid y)} = \text{BF}_{+/-0} \times \frac{\Pr(H_{+})}{\Pr(H_{-0})} where \text{BF}_{+/-0} quantifies the relative likelihood of the data under H_{+} versus H_{-0}. Under prior symmetry, \frac{\Pr(H_{+})}{\Pr(H_{-0})} = 1, which yields \frac{pd}{1 - pd} = \text{BF}_{+/-0} (Casella & Berger, 1987; Marsman & Wagenmakers, 2017). Thus, the odds \frac{pd}{1 - pd} computed from posterior draws can be interpreted directly as evidence favoring \theta > 0 over \theta \le 0 (or \theta < 0 over \theta \ge 0).

Multiplicity adjustment via prior-odds

Assessing a single directional claim under a symmetric prior centered at \theta_{\text{null}} corresponds to prior indifference (i.e., a prior probability of 0.5). If m directional claims are considered jointly and treated as independent a priori, assigning a probability of 0.5 to each claim induces a joint prior that quickly assigns high probability to the event that at least one effect is in the claimed direction.

Specifically, the prior probability that none of the m effects is in the claimed direction is (0.5)^m, so the prior probability that at least one is in the claimed direction is 1 - (0.5)^m. For example, with m = 10, \Pr(\text{none in claimed direction}) = 0.5^{10} \approx 0.001, and \Pr(\text{at least one in claimed direction}) = 1 - 0.5^{10} \approx 0.999.

In this setting, a principled response to multiplicity is to reduce the prior odds assigned to any particular directional claim as a function of m. Because the prior is symmetric regarding the sign of \theta, and pd is defined as the posterior probability of the dominant direction, the same prior odds adjustment applies whether the data favor \theta > 0 or \theta < 0.

Concretely, for each test i = 1, \dots, m, let \Pr(H_{1, i}) denote the multiplicity-adjusted prior probability of the favored direction, and let \Pr(H_{0, i}) denote the prior probability of its complement (i.e., the opposite direction, with 0 included), where \Pr(H_{0, i}) + \Pr(H_{1, i}) = 1. Then the adjusted posterior odds satisfy: \frac{pd_{\mathrm{adj}, i}}{1 - pd_{\mathrm{adj}, i}} = \frac{pd_i}{1 - pd_i} \times \frac{\Pr(H_{1, i})}{\Pr(H_{0, i})} or equivalently, pd_{\mathrm{adj}, i} = \frac{pd_i \Pr(H_{1, i})}{pd_i \Pr(H_{1, i}) + (1 - pd_i) \Pr(H_{0, i})} Thus, multiplicity acts only through the prior odds factor \Pr(H_{1, i}) / \Pr(H_{0, i}).

Choosing the prior odds

The multiplicity adjustment operates entirely through the prior odds. To specify these prior odds in a way that scales appropriately with the number of tests, we follow the global null formulation of Jeffreys (1938) (see also Westfall et al. (1997)). Conceptually, we first specify a prior probability for the global null: the event that all individual null hypotheses are true. We then work backward from this joint probability to derive the implied prior probability for each individual test.

Formally, for each test i = 1, \dots, m, let H_{1, i} be the directional claim corresponding to the posterior-dominant sign (i.e., the direction used to compute pd_i = \Pr(H_{1, i} \mid y)), and let H_{0, i} denote its complement (which includes zero). Note that H_{1, i} is defined post hoc based on the observed posterior direction.

The global null hypothesis, \mathbf{H_0}, is the event that none of the m parameters lie in their respective favored directions: \mathbf{H_0} = \bigcap_{i=1}^m H_{0, i}

Assuming prior independence across hypotheses and a common per-hypothesis prior probability \Pr(H_{1, i}) = k, the probability of the global null is given by \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = (1 - k)^m. Solving for k yields the adjusted prior probability for each individual test:

k = 1 - \Pr(\mathbf{H_0})^{1/m}

This formulation ensures the multiplicity penalty scales logically. For example, if a researcher tests m = 5 hypotheses and assigns a prior probability of \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = 0.5 to the global null, the prior probability for any single test finding an effect in the expected direction becomes k = 1 - 0.5^{1/5} \approx 0.129. As the number of tests (m) increases, k decreases, rendering the prior increasingly skeptical of any single claim (Figure 4).

Researchers can encode their knowledge by selecting \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}). When few non-null effects are anticipated, \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) should be set relatively high (e.g., 0.4); conversely, when effects are well established, \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) can be lower (e.g., 0.1). Once \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) is fixed, larger families necessarily imply a smaller k, thereby placing a higher prior probability on the null for each individual test (Figure 4).

However, a boundary condition arises when the family of tests is small (m) and researchers hold a strong a priori expectation of non-null effects (i.e., a very low \Pr(\mathbf{H_0})). Under these conditions, the calibration can become anti-conservative: the formula may assign a prior probability k > 0.5 to the favored direction (Figure 4, left panel). Because this would artificially inflate the posterior odds rather than penalizing them (Figure 4, right panel), we impose a strict upper bound. If the formula yields k > 0.5, we cap it at 0.5 (ensuring \Pr(H_{0, i}) \ge 0.5). This guarantees the adjustment acts exclusively as a conservative safeguard against false positives, leaving the posterior probabilities unchanged from their unadjusted baseline when no penalty is warranted.

Simulation Study 1

To illustrate the impact of \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) on both FWER and power, we simulated two data-generating regimes: a global-null scenario (\theta_1 = \dots = \theta_m = 0) and an alternative scenario where exactly one effect is non-null (\theta_1 = \dots = \theta_{m-1} = 0, \theta_m = 0.3). Replicating our previous simulation procedure with a fixed sample size of n = 50, we varied the global null prior across \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) \in \{0.50, 0.40, 0.30, 0.20, 0.10\}. Following Westfall et al. (1997), we compared this Bayesian multiplicity adjustment with the Bonferroni p-value correction, which provides strong family-wise error rate (FWER) control.

Of foremost importance, if highly skeptical priors are already employed for estimation (e.g., \tau = 0.2), applying this multiplicity adjustment constitutes a restrictive “double” correction. Beyond this case, the adjusted pd procedure offers a flexible trade-off governed by the global null prior parameter, \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}). An area of equivalence emerges when setting \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = 0.4, which produces FWER and power trajectories that closely mirror the Bonferroni adjustment across varying family sizes (Figure 5). This empirical alignment is consistent with the asymptotic proof by Westfall et al. (1997) demonstrating that a global null probability of \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = e^{-1} \approx 0.368 reproduces Bonferroni-like behavior. Thus, selecting \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) \approx 0.4 effectively calibrates the Bayesian prior to match the severity of the frequentist \alpha correction.

The simulation results further reveal that when researchers hold a strong expectation of non-null effects (e.g., \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) \leq 0.2), the Bayesian adjustment yields higher power than the Bonferroni correction but suffers from FWER inflation, particularly as the prior scale (\tau) increase (Figure 5). Conversely, imposing a highly skeptical global prior (\Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = 0.5) over-corrects the FWER to well below the nominal 0.05 threshold, correspondingly sacrificing power (Figure 6).

Lastly, although setting \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) \in \{0.2, 0.3\} yields FWER and power trajectories distinct from the Bonferroni method, these priors still provide substantial error control. Specifically, they maintain a maximum FWER of roughly 10% (only 5% above the nominal level, with m = 3 and \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) =0.2) while delivering a notable power increase of approximately +15%.

Accounting for correlation

A limitation of the proposed procedure is that it allocates the global-null probability across m hypotheses under an independence assumption. When hypotheses are correlated, for instance because parameters are estimated jointly within a single model or because measures share the same observations, independence-based multiplicity adjustments tend to be overly conservative (Pesarin & Salmaso, 2010; Westfall & Young, 1993).

One principled remedy is to replace the nominal number of tests m with an effective number m_{\text{eff}} that reflects the amount of unique information in the family. This requires estimating a correlation matrix \mathbf{R} among the tests (e.g., among posterior distributions) and mapping it to m_{\text{eff}} via an eigenvalue decomposition. Several methods for this mapping have been proposed, each resting on different assumptions (Cheverud, 2001; Li & Ji, 2005; see Del Giudice, 2021 for a critical discussion); here we adopt the eigenvalue-based formula of Cheverud (2001). This correction can be applied post hoc given only an estimated or assumed correlation matrix1.

Simulation Study 2

To evaluate this approach in correlated settings, we extended the simulation study to account for dependence structures. Because the procedure relies on the estimated correlation, we varied the family size m \in \{2, 4, 6, 10, 20\} and the sample size n \in \{30, 50, 100\} to illustrate how estimation uncertainty in the correlation propagates into the correction. Each condition was replicated 100,000 times.

In each replication, data were drawn from a multivariate normal distribution with an equicorrelation structure: y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}), where \mu_1 = \cdots = \mu_{m/2} = 0 (null hypotheses) and \mu_{m/2+1} = \cdots = \mu_m = 0.3 (non-null hypotheses), so that exactly half the hypotheses were true nulls. The off-diagonal entries of \mathbf{R} were set to a common value \rho \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}.

Posterior distributions were obtained using a multivariate normal conjugate model with prior mean \mu = 0 and prior standard deviation \tau = 1.5, a specification under which Bayesian and frequentist inference coincide. The prior weight on the global null was fixed at \Pr(\mathbf{H_0}) = 0.4, consistent with earlier results. Performance was evaluated in terms of FWER within the null subset and average power, defined as the proportion of correctly identified non-null hypotheses (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995; Izmirlian et al., 2025).

We compared the pd-based multiplicity correction against the Holm procedure as a frequentist family-wise error rate benchmark (Holm, 1979). Holm was selected over Bonferroni because it provides strong FWER control while achieving greater power, and it is among the most widely implemented correction procedures in applied research.

As shown in Figure 7, independence-based corrections are overconservative when tests are correlated, as is well known, leading to a substantial loss of power that widens with increasing family size m. By contrast, the m_{\text{eff}}-based procedure maintained FWER near the nominal level while retaining greater power across most conditions, with the advantage growing as \rho increased. An exception was observed at small sample sizes, where possible imprecise estimation of the correlation structure led to small FWER inflation, suggesting that the reliability of the m_{\text{eff}} estimate is a key determinant of the procedure’s calibration.

Application: worked example with the R Package multibayes

The procedure is implemented in the multibayes R package, which provides functions for computing adjusted pd values from either raw posterior draws or a vector of unadjusted pd values. The core function, pd.adjust(), accepts posterior draws directly and computes pd values internally when needed. The output includes the adjusted pd values, the tested null value, the implied global null probability, and the number of tests m (reported as the effective number of tests when the correlation structure among parameters is accounted for). Unadjusted pd values are retained alongside adjusted ones to facilitate direct comparison; when posterior draws are provided as input, the posterior mean for each parameter is also returned.

For this example, we use the openly available dataset from Lin et al. (2020), who investigated the effect of ego depletion on conflict resolution across four studies. In their paradigm, an initial high-demand (vs. low-demand) task was followed by a Stroop task. For simplicity, we focus on Study 1, which tested the effects of congruency and ego depletion in a sample of 253 participants (total observations: 91,080; nested within subjects). The dataset is readily accessible via the afex package (Singmann et al., 2016) and was chosen for this reason; this example is not intended as a critique of the original work.

Given the repeated-measures structure of the data and the large number of trials per participant, we fit multilevel generalized linear models using brms (Bürkner, 2017), estimating both population-level and participant-specific effects. We evaluated the effects of congruency (congruent vs. incongruent), condition (depletion vs. control), and their interaction on both reaction time (RT) and accuracy (ACC).2 A lognormal model was specified for RT data, and a binomial logistic regression for ACC. Default flat priors in brms were adopted not as a principled recommendation, but to illustrate the method’s behavior under the most common applied scenario, which also represents the condition where the prior-odds adjustment is most appropriate.

Although one could debate whether pd values should be corrected jointly across both models or separately within each, we adopt the joint approach, as the prior over the hypothesis space spans all six hypotheses. Distribution specific (e.g., sigma for the Lognormal model), and subject-specific parameters are treated as nuisance, as they are not considered for inference.

For reaction times, H_1 and H_2 predict positive effects of congruency and depletion condition, tested against non-zero null values of practical interest (\theta_{null} = 0.05 and \theta_{null} = 0, respectively); H_3 examines the interaction exploratorily with a direction-agnostic test. The accuracy hypotheses mirror this structure: H_4 and H_5 predict negative effects of congruency and condition (\theta_{null} = -1 and \theta_{null} = 0), while H_6 treats the interaction as exploratory. For directional hypotheses, pd is the posterior probability in the predicted direction beyond the null value; for exploratory ones, pd = \max\!\big(\Pr(\hat\theta > 0),\, \Pr(\hat\theta < 0)\big).

For the RT analysis, we retained only correct trials preceded by a correct trial (i.e., error and post-error trials were excluded), yielding 76,397 observations. Three chains of 10,000 iterations each were run.

data_rt <- data_stroop |>
  group_by(id) |>
  # remove error and post error trials
  filter(acc == 1 & lag(acc) == 1) |> ungroup()

mod_rt <- brm(formula = rt ~ 1 + congruency*condition + 
                            (1 + congruency*condition || id),
                family = lognormal(),
                cores = 3, chains = 3, iter = 10000,
                data = data_rt)

For the ACC analysis, omission trials were already excluded during data loading via na.omit(). Data were aggregated to subject \times condition \times congruency cells prior to modeling, yielding 1,012 rows (253 subjects \times 4 cells). Three chains of 10,000 iterations each were run.

The function was called with q = 0.4, mu0 = c(0.05, 0, 0, -1, 0, 0), direction = c(1, 1, 0, -1, -1, 0), and R = TRUE, yielding an effective number of hypotheses of m \approx 5.70, which reflects the estimated correlations among posterior draws. The mu0 argument specifies hypothesis-specific null values (here \theta_\text{null} = 0.05 for congruency_rt and \theta_\text{null} = -1 for congruency_acc, with zero elsewhere), while direction encodes the predicted sign for each test (+1 for positive, -1 for negative, and 0 for agnostic).

Five of the six hypotheses remained strongly supported after adjustment: the congruency effects on both reaction time (pd_\text{adj} = 1.000) and accuracy (pd_\text{adj} = 1.000), the condition effect on accuracy (pd_\text{adj} = 0.999), and both interaction effects (pd_\text{adj} = 0.986 and pd_\text{adj} = 0.992, respectively). The condition effect on reaction time showed no support (pd = pd_\text{adj} = 0.500): since a positive direction was specified (direction = 1), the raw directional probability pd_\text{raw} = Pr(\hat{\theta} > 0) \approx 0.468 indicates that the posterior was concentrated opposite to the predicted direction. Both pd and pd_\text{adj} are floored at 0.5 for directional tests, so this minimum value reflects absence of support for the predicted direction rather than evidence against it. The floor prevents the adjustment from amplifying contradictory evidence and ensures a consistent interpretation across all tests.

Discussion

Thresholding pd into a decision rule and applying that rule repeatedly across many parameters induces a multiplicity problem. Without adjustment, researchers implicitly accept a prior probability for the global null hypothesis that may be far lower than intended. This concern is particularly acute when non-informative or weakly informative priors are used, or when sufficient data render the likelihood dominant. The procedure proposed here targets this decision-level problem by extending the foundational work of Jeffreys (1938) and Westfall et al. (1997) to the pd context, allowing researcher-specific knowledge to enter transparently through the prior-odds specification. Researchers can explicitly encode their expectations about the prevalence of true effects through the prior probability assigned to the global null hypothesis, separating within-model priors on parameters from prior assumptions over the hypothesis space. In this sense, the procedure is not merely a correction for multiple testing, but a transparent mechanism for connecting prior beliefs to decision-making under multiplicity.

Related corrections have been implemented for specific testing contexts, such as post-hoc tests in JASP (Jong, 2019; van den Bergh et al., 2020). The present procedure extends this logic to pd, accounts for correlations among tested hypotheses, and provides a general-purpose implementation for applied use. Unlike existing tools tied to particular model types or software environments, it applies to pd values arising from different modeling strategoes. While the most coherent approach estimates all relevant parameters within a single joint model that explicitly represents their dependence (Gelman et al., 2012, 2013; Kruschke, 2011), this is not always feasible in practice. Researchers sometimes report pd values from multiple separate analyses within the same manuscript (Blondé et al., 2021; Ciranka et al., 2026), and the procedure is designed to remain applicable in this setting. The level of information required is flexible: when a joint model is available, its posterior draws can be passed directly to pd.adjust(), which computes the correlation structure automatically and uses it to derive the effective number of tests. When only separate models are available, draws from each can be combined into a single matrix and passed to the function, which then estimates the joint correlation structure across all parameters. When posterior draws are unavailable, the user can supply a precomputed correlation matrix, assumed correlations for specific parameter pairs, or a single scalar representing the expected mean correlation across tests. When no correlation information is provided, the adjustment proceeds under an independence assumption.

A practical consideration when applying the adjustment is the definition of the family of hypotheses, which Shaffer (1995) identifies as “the first and perhaps most crucial decision”. In broad terms, Hochberg and Tamhane (1987) define a family as “any collection of inferences for which it is meaningful to take into account some combined measure of errors”. Westfall et al. (2011) operationalize this by specifying that a family is “a set of questions of interest that meet one or more of these criteria:

  • The questions form a natural and coherent unit.
  • The questions are considered simultaneously in the decision-making process.
  • The questions form a set over which you play ‘pick the winner’.”

Regardless of the specific definition adopted, we recommend reporting the choice transparently.

An important caveat is that this framework does not provide evidence for a point null hypothesis, that is, it does not quantify posterior support for the null value itself. Instead, it evaluates directional claims based on inequalities (e.g., \theta > \theta_\text{null} vs. \theta \le \theta_\text{null}). By construction, the complementary hypothesis includes the null value, but the framework does not assign it special status. When the null value falls outside the credible interval, one can infer its relative implausibility as a candidate value, but this differs fundamentally from quantifying evidence in its favor. Researchers whose primary interest lies in null evidence are better served by other Bayes factor approaches (Dienes, 2016; Heck et al., 2023; Ly et al., 2016; Morey & Rouder, 2011).

Several further limitations should be noted. The formal correspondence between pd and the frequentist p-value holds under specific conditions namely: continuous posteriors, sufficiently large samples, and diffuse priors, and may not generalize to small-sample settings or analyses with strongly informative priors. The m_\text{eff} correction additionally requires estimating or assuming the correlation structure among tests; misspecification of this structure will propagate into the adjustment. Finally, the framework is developed specifically for pd, but extending the prior-odds logic to other posterior indices is a natural direction for future work. For example, the prior-odds logic could in principle be extended to Bayes factors comparing null and alternative intervals, in which case evidence for the null could be derived.

We recommend that researchers explicitly justify the choice of the prior probability that the global null holds, by drawing on theory, expert elicitation, previous findings, or domain expertise, rather than treating it as an arbitrary tuning parameter (O’Hagan, 2019). For instance, in an exploratory cognitive paradigm with several uncertain hypotheses, a researcher might consider the global null moderately plausible; in a confirmatory setting with strong prior evidence, a lower value would be more appropriate. For applied reporting, the unadjusted pd should be reported for each parameter alongside at least one multiplicity-adjusted measure based on the proposed prior-odds correction, with the number of tests and the implied prior probability clearly stated.

As Bayesian methods continue to be adopted in psychological research, often in pragmatic settings where default priors and conventional thresholds are the norm, tools that make the error consequences of such practices explicit become increasingly necessary. The procedure developed here provides a coherent and practical way to recalibrate pd-based decisions for multiple tests, making reported claims less likely to reflect chance patterns across many parameters.
