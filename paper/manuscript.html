Abstract
| Bayesian methods are increasingly adopted in psychological research for practical reasons, such as computational flexibility and intuitive parameter summaries, without necessarily endorsing subjective Bayesian philosophy. When not fully committed to a subjectivist stance, researchers may wish to evaluate their inferential procedures by operating characteristics. This consideration becomes particularly relevant when posterior indices like the probability of direction (pd) are thresholded across many parameters. The pd is the posterior probability that a parameter is strictly greater or smaller than a null value. Here, we examine the operating characteristics of pd-based decisions under repeated use, leveraging its correspondence with the frequentist p-value in common settings. Simulations under the global null show that extreme pd values can occur by chance and that familywise error rates increase with the number of tests, exhibiting behavior comparable to uncorrected p-values. We reformulate pd as posterior odds and propose a prior-odds adjustment following Jeffreys’ and Westfall’s approach to multiplicity. The adjustment reduces the prior probability assigned to each hypothesis as family size increases and can be calibrated to reflect prior beliefs about the global null. We extend the method to account for correlations among hypotheses. An R package implements the procedure.

The primary message of this paper for Bayesians is that they should calibrate their priors according to their beliefs about \(Pr(H_{0i} \text{ is true, all } i)\): their marginal probabilities \(Pr(H_{0i})\) might be much larger than they had thought. [@Westfall1997, p.426]

Over the past two decades, the proportion of articles reporting Bayesian analyses in psychology has increased considerably [@Van2017; @Jevremov2024; @Pfadt2025]. Unfortunately, this expansion has been accompanied by practices reminiscent of the “null ritual” in frequentist statistics, where widespread use was associated with automatic application rather than principled reasoning [@Gigerenzer2004; @Cohen1994]. For instance, researchers sometimes treat Bayes Factor thresholds (e.g., BF > 3) as dichotomous decision rules or claim that an effect “exists” because the posterior probability of a positive effect exceeds a conventional benchmark such as 95% [@Heck2023; @Kelter2023; @Tendeiro2024; @Hoijtink2016; @Tendeiro2019; @Kruschke2018]. While such summaries are convenient, their use as default cutoffs encourages all-or-none interpretations. When these binary judgments are applied repeatedly across many parameters, the issue of multiplicity arises.

In the frequentist framework, multiplicity is typically framed as Type I error (false-positive) problem: if each individual test is conducted at level \(\alpha\), the probability of at least one false positive increases with the number of tests. Classical approaches therefore control either the family-wise error rate (the probability of at least one Type I error) or the false discovery rate (the expected proportion of false positives among rejected hypotheses) [@Benjamini1995; @Westfall1993]. Additionally, when researchers examine many parameters but selectively report only the most extreme estimates (i.e., those that reach statistical significance), selection effects can produce exaggerated magnitudes, a problem known as Type M error [@Gelman2000; @Zwet2021; @Altoe2020]. Bayesian shrinkage methods address these distortions by pulling extreme estimates toward more plausible values. Such shrinkage can arise through hierarchical models with partial pooling [@Gelman2012; @GelmanBook; @Kruschke2011; @Berry1999] and/or through informative priors that reduce overestimation in low signal-to-noise settings [@Zwet2022; @Dawid1994]. Shrinkage reduces Type M errors, but it does not, on its own, provide a guarantee about the error properties of a repeated decision rule applied across many parameters. In particular, if the same cutoff is applied to many parameters (e.g., declaring an effect whenever posterior probability exceeds 95%), the chance of at least one spurious finding can remain inflated, even if shrinkage reduces it. Bayesian approaches that directly target this issue typically rely on either simultaneous credible statements [@Gelman2000; @Pruzek2016; @Schnell2020; @Box1992] or priors on the hypothesis/model space that scale with the number of tested effects [@Scott2006; @Scott2010; @Jeffreys1938; @Westfall1997]. Nevertheless, in psychological research shrinkage is often treated as sufficient for multiplicity control [@Basket2015; @Limbachia2021; @Chen2020; @Hogeveen2022; @Mathar2022].

Other justifications invokes the subjectivist interpretation of Bayesian inference, which treats posterior probabilities as rational degrees of personal belief [@Lindley1972; @Edwards1963; @Rozeboom1960]. However, this interpretation fits poorly with common psychological practice, where researchers typically apply default software priors (often weakly informative) rather than priors encoding substantive domain knowledge [@Van2017; @Smid2020]. When these priors are combined with sufficient data, Bayesian and frequentist procedures often converge to similar conclusions [@Bernardo1994; @GelmanBook; @Albers2018].

Given this convergence and the prevailing practices in psychological research, we aim to evaluate the frequentist operating characteristics of Bayesian decision rules when examining many parameters. Specifically, we focus on the Probability of Direction (pd), a recently proposed and increasingly adopted Bayesian index that can be explicitly linked to the frequentist p-value in common settings [@Marsman2017; @Berger1987; @Casella1987; @Makowski2019] [more REFs]. This connection allows for straightforward evaluation of pd-based decision rules under repeated use, enabling direct comparison between Bayesian and frequentist approaches to multiplicity control. We revisit the solution proposed by @Westfall1997, reframing it as a transparent, practical framework that encourages more deliberate reporting and highlights the challenges of inference across many parameters.

The paper proceeds in four steps. First, we introduce pd and make its connection to the frequentist p-value explicit. Second, we characterize the null behavior of pd and, via simulations, examine the multiple-testing properties of pd-thresholded decisions, with a focus on family-wise error rate. Third, we express pd in terms of posterior odds, relate these odds to directional Bayes factors, and clarify the role of prior odds. Finally, building on Jeffreys’ global-null formulation [@Jeffreys1938] and the Bayesian justification for Bonferroni-style multiplicity adjustment developed by Westfall, Johnson, and Utts [@Westfall1997], we show how these prior-odds ideas extend naturally to pd. In the discussion, we offer practical guidance for implementation and reporting.

Probability of Direction

The probability of direction (pd) is a descriptive posterior index that quantifies how strongly the posterior distribution favors one direction of an effect relative to a reference (\(\theta_{null}\)) value [@Makowski2019]. For a continuous posterior, it can be defined as the larger of the two one-sided posterior probabilities: \(pd = \max(\Pr(\theta < \theta_{null} \mid y),\ \Pr(\theta > \theta_{null} \mid y)),\) and it ranges from 0.50 (no directional preference) to 1 (all on one side).

Because pd is derived from the posterior, it inherits dependence on the full model specification (likelihood and prior), so the long-run error behavior of pd-based decisions is not a property of pd “in isolation” but of the overall Bayesian procedure that generates the posterior.

Probability of Direction as a test of existence and direction

A convenient starting point for calibration is the mapping between pd and the frequentist p-value. Under flat (or sufficiently diffuse symmetric) priors in typical hypothesis-testing scenarios (e.g., testing means), pd is the posterior probability of the data-favored sign relative to \(\theta_{null}\). This value corresponds to the complement of the one-sided p-value [@Marsman2017; @Casella1987], with the two-sided relation approximated by \(p \approx 2(1 - pd)\). In practice, the pd is often used to summarize both the direction and existence of an effect [REFs].

This interpretation is clearest when phrased as a decision rule \(pd>c\): the posterior must assign more than \(c\) probability mass to the side of \(\theta_{\text{null}}\) favored by the data. Setting \(c=1-\alpha/2\) is convenient because it bounds the posterior mass in the opposite tail by \(\alpha/2\). For concreteness, suppose the posterior favors a positive effect so that \(pd=\Pr(\theta>\theta_{\text{null}}\mid y)\). Then \(pd>c\) implies \[ \Pr(\theta\le \theta_{\text{null}}\mid y) =1-\Pr(\theta>\theta_{\text{null}}\mid y) <1-c =\alpha/2. \] With \(\alpha=0.05\), this gives \(c=0.975\): the rule \(pd>0.975\) requires that \(\Pr(\theta\le \theta_{\text{null}}\mid y)<0.025\), placing less than 2.5% of posterior mass at or below \(\theta_{\text{null}}\).

Let \(q_p\) denote the \(p\)-th posterior quantile, so the central \((1-\alpha)\) equi-tailed credible interval is \([q_{\alpha/2},\,q_{1-\alpha/2}]\), which leaves probability \(\alpha/2\) in each tail. The condition \(\Pr(\theta\le \theta_{\text{null}}\mid y)<\alpha/2\) holds if and only if \(\theta_{\text{null}}\) falls below \(q_{\alpha/2}\), that is, when \(\theta_{\text{null}}\notin [q_{\alpha/2},\,q_{1-\alpha/2}]\). Thus, observing \(pd>0.975\) is equivalent to excluding \(\theta_{\text{null}}\) from the central 95% equi-tailed credible interval (see Figure 1). When the credible interval excludes the null value, one can infer that the null is relatively implausible (@Box1992).

Given these considerations, although pd and the frequentist p-value target different quantities, it is expected that decision rules of the form \(pd>c\) approximate the operating characteristics of familiar frequentist rules such as \(p<\alpha\).

Simulation study

In our simulations we generate data under the global null (all \(\theta=0\)) and we compute the frequency of \(pd > c\), where \(c = 0.975\) (\(\alpha = 0.05\)). Operationally, this quantifies how often the procedure falsely excludes zero from the 95% central credible interval (bounded by \((1-c)\) and \(c\)), that is, incorrectly concludes that an effect exists (with a particular direction).

Although pd is a measure of direction, pure directional testing conditions on the assumption that \(\theta \neq 0\) and evaluates only the sign of the effect by comparing \(H_{+}\) versus \(H_{-}\); \(H_{0}:\theta=0\) is not among the hypotheses under consideration. In contrast, here we explicitly include \(H_{0}\) [@Goeman2010], focusing on decisions about both existence and direction (when the interval excludes zero, e.g., \(H_{+}\)) rather than decisions about direction only (when the interval includes zero, e.g., \(H_{0+}\)).

Specifically, we conduct a Monte Carlo simulation in which, for each condition, we generated families of size \(m \in \{1,2,5,10,20\}\), with \(n \in \{30,50,100\}\) observations per dataset, and repeated the procedure \(10{,}000\) times. In every replication, data were generated under the global null: each dataset had true mean 0 and unit variance, \(y \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). For each dataset, we compute the posterior distribution of \(\theta\) assuming \(y_i \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)\) and \(\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)\), with \(\tau \in \{0.2, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2\}\) ranging from skeptical to relatively diffuse priors.

In most applications, the pd is estimated from posterior draws as the larger of the two one-sided posterior probabilities: the proportion of draws above \(\theta_{\text{null}}\) and the proportion below \(\theta_{\text{null}}\). Because this is a Monte Carlo estimate, its numerical resolution depends directly on the number of posterior draws [@Makowski2019].

In our simulation, however, the conjugate normal–normal model implies a Gaussian posterior, \(\theta \mid y \sim \mathcal N(\mu_{\text{post}},\,\sigma_{\text{post}}^{2}),\) allowing pd to be computed in closed form. Specifically, \[ pd=\max(\Pr(\theta>\theta_{null}\mid y),\Pr(\theta<\theta_{null}\mid y)) =\Phi\!\left(\left|\frac{\mu_{\text{post}}-\theta_{null}}{\sigma_{\text{post}}}\right|\right), \] with \(\theta_{\text{null}}=0\). For the frequentist comparison, we conducted two-sided one-sample \(z\)-tests (\(\alpha = 0.05\)).

Prior influence

Under the null hypothesis, p-values are uniformly distributed on \([0,1]\), and because pd can be mapped to the p-value, this implies a corresponding null reference behavior for pd on \([0.5,1]\). However, because the pd is a posterior probability about the sign of \(\theta\), its null distribution is not determined by the likelihood alone: it can shift depending on how strongly the prior concentrates probability near 0 (i.e., how readily the prior “expects” near-null effects). Showing this prior dependence at \(m=1\) makes the target of later multiplicity analyses explicit: even before any multiplicity is introduced, a fixed cutoff such as \(pd > 0.975\) does not have a universal “null meaning” across priors and sample sizes.

As shown in Figure 2, pd exhibits behavior broadly analogous to a p-value when samples are large and the prior places relatively little mass near 0. However, increasing prior skepticism (i.e., concentrating more prior mass around 0) reduces the frequency of extreme pd values.

Behavior under multiplicity

Building on this baseline, multiplicity can be introduced as a second, distinct mechanism: even when each individual test has only a small probability of producing an extreme pd under \(H_{0}\), the probability that at least one among \(pd_{1},\ldots,pd_{m}\) is extreme increases with the number of tests \(m\). We quantify this familywise error rate (FWER) behavior by estimating the frequency of \(pd > 0.975\) for families ranging from \(m=1\) to \(m=20\).

As expected, pd-based decisions behaves broadly analogously to their frequentist counterpart when sample sizes are large and the prior places relatively little mass near 0 (Figure 3). By contrast, as the prior becomes more skeptical (i.e., increasingly concentrated around 0), the FWER decreases.

The simulation results suggest that Type I error rates could be reduced by adopting more skeptical within-model priors for each parameter. However, using within-model prior skepticism as a general remedy for multiplicity is problematic because it conflates two distinct goals: (i) encoding substantive prior knowledge about a specific parameter and (ii) calibrating the error rate of a decision rule under repeated testing.

When the primary aim is error-rate control, adjusting the within-model prior introduces bias into parameter estimates as an unintended side effect. The approach presented below instead adjusts the prior odds assigned to the multiple hypotheses while keeping the within-model prior for any single parameter unchanged. This separation allows researchers to choose the most appropriate prior for each parameter based on substantive knowledge and modeling considerations, while still controlling error rates when making simultaneous statements about multiple parameters.

Importantly, the method is valid if and only if the within-model prior is centered symmetrically around the tested null value. That is, the within-model prior must allow the parameter to take values both greater than and smaller than the null value with equal prior plausibility, rather than constraining the parameter space to only the expected direction. Subject to this symmetry requirement, researchers retain full flexibility in prior specification.

Probability of direction, posterior odds, and prior-odds adjustment

From posterior probabilities to posterior odds

To connect \(pd\) to posterior odds, consider the directional hypotheses \(H_{+}:\theta>0\) and \(H_{-}:\theta<0\). These hypotheses partition the parameter space based on the sign of \(\theta\). If the prior for \(\theta\) is continuous and symmetric about 0, then the two directions are equally plausible a priori: \(\Pr(H_{+})=\Pr(H_{-})=1/2\). Without loss of generality, assume the posterior favors \(H_{+}\), so that \(pd=\Pr(H_{+}\mid y)=\Pr(\theta>0\mid y)\), then the complement is given by \(1-pd=\Pr(\theta\le0\mid y)\). The posterior odds comparing these two hypothesis are therefore: \[\frac{pd}{1-pd}=\frac{\Pr(H_{+}\mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-0}\mid y)}.\] For a continuous posterior distribution, the probability of any single point (e.g., \(\Pr(\theta= 0\mid y)\)) is 0, which means that one could write \(\frac{pd}{1-pd}=\frac{\Pr(H_{+}\mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-}\mid y)}\). However, we retain the notation \(H_{-0}\) in the denominator to explicitly indicate that the hypothesis includes the point null value of zero.

Applying Bayes’ rule, these posterior odds can be decomposed into a Bayes Factor and prior odds: \[ \frac{\Pr(H_{+}\mid y)}{\Pr(H_{-0}\mid y)} = \text{BF}_{+/-0}\times\frac{\Pr(H_{+})}{\Pr(H_{-0})}, \] where \(\text{BF}{+/-0}\) quantifies the relative likelihood of the data under \(H+\) versus \(H_{-0}\). Under prior symmetry, \(\frac{\Pr(H_{+})}{\Pr(H_{-0})} \approx 1\), which yields \(\frac{pd}{1-pd}=\text{BF}_{+/-0}\) [@Marsman2017; @Casella1987]. Thus, the odds \(\frac{pd}{(1-pd)}\) computed from posterior draws can be interpreted directly as evidence favoring \(\theta>0\) over \(\theta \le 0\) (or \(\theta<0\) over \(\theta \geq 0\)).

Multiplicity adjustment via post-hoc prior-odds

Assessing a single directional claim under a symmetric prior centered at \(\theta_{null}\) corresponds to prior indifference (i.e., \(1/2\)). If \(m\) directional claims are considered simultaneously and treated as independent a priori, assigning probability \(1/2\) to each claim induces a joint prior that quickly assigns high probability to the event that at least one effect is in the claimed direction. Specifically, the prior probability that none of the \(m\) effects is in the claimed direction is \((1/2)^m\), so the prior probability that at least one is in the claimed direction is \(1-(1/2)^m\). For example, with \(m=10\), \(\Pr(\text{none in claimed direction})=0.5^{10}\approx 0.001\), and \(\Pr(\text{at least one in claimed direaction})=1-0.5^{10}\approx 0.999\).

In this setting, a principled response to multiplicity is to reduce the prior odds assigned to any particular directional claim as a function of \(m\). Because the prior is symmetric in the sign of \(\theta\) and \(pd\) is defined as the posterior probability of the dominant direction, the same prior-odds adjustment applies whether the data favor \(\theta>0\) or \(\theta<0\).

Concretely, for each test \(i=1,\dots,m\), let \(\Pr(H_{1_i})\) denote the multiplicity-adjusted prior probability of the favored direction and let \(\Pr(H_{0_i})\) denote the prior probability of its complement (i.e., the opposite direction, with \(0\) included), with \(\Pr(H_{0_i})+\Pr(H_{1_i})=1\). Then the adjusted posterior odds satisfy \[ \frac{pd_{\mathrm{adj},i}}{1-pd_{\mathrm{adj},i}} = \frac{pd_i}{1-pd_i}\times \frac{\Pr(H_{1_i})}{\Pr(H_{0_i})}, \] or equivalently, \[ pd_{\mathrm{adj},i} = \frac{pd_i\,\Pr(H_{1_i})}{pd_i\,\Pr(H_{1_i}) + (1-pd_i)\,\Pr(H_{0_i})}. \] Thus, multiplicity acts only through the prior-odds factor \(\Pr(H_{1_i})/\Pr(H_{0_i})\).

Choosing the prior-odds

The multiplicity adjustment above operates entirely through the prior-odds factor \(\Pr(H_{1,i})/\Pr(H_{0,i})\). To specify these prior odds in a way that scales with the number of tests, we follow the global-null formulation of @Jeffreys1938 (see also @Westfall1997).

Let \(Q\) denote the global null event that none of the \(m\) parameters lies in its data-favored direction, and let \(Q^c\) denote its complement (i.e., at least one parameter lies in its data-favored direction). We assign equal prior probability to these events, \(\Pr(Q)=\Pr(Q^c)=0.5\).

For each test \(i=1,\dots,m\), define \(H_{1,i}\) as the directional claim corresponding to the posterior-dominant sign (i.e., the direction used in \(pd_i=\Pr(H_{1,i}\mid y)\)), and let \(H_{0,i}\) denote its complement (the opposite direction, with \(0\) included). With these definitions, \[ Q=\bigcap_{i=1}^m H_{0,i}. \]

Assuming prior independence across tests and a common per-hypothesis prior probability \(\Pr(H_{1,i}) = k\), it follows that \(\Pr(Q)=(1-k)^m\). Imposing \(\Pr(Q)=0.5\) yields \((1-k)^m=0.5\), and therefore \[ \Pr(H_{1_i}) = k \;=\; 1-0.5^{1/m}. \] As \(m\) increases, \(k=\Pr(H_{1,i})\) decreases, encoding increasing skepticism about any particular directional claim (see Figure 4).

Following @Westfall1997, we compare this method with the Bonferroni p-value adjustment (\(p_{adj} = p \times m\)), which gives strong frequentist family-wise error rate control.

Although grounded in Bayesian prior-odds logic, the procedure exhibits behavior similar to frequentist multiplicity correction. However, it tends to be more conservative, even when both \(n\) and \(\tau\) are large (Figure 5). Importantly, when skeptical priors are already used for estimation, applying the multiplicity adjustment constitutes a “double” correction.

Having linked the multiplicity adjustment to the global null event \(Q\), the same construction yields a more general calibration directly in terms of \(\Pr(Q)\). Specifically, instead of fixing \(\Pr(Q)=0.5\), researchers may encode background knowledge by choosing \(\Pr(Q) = q\). As stated above, under a priori independence and a common per-hypothesis prior probability \(k=\Pr(H_{1,i})\) for the favored-direction alternative, \(\Pr(Q)=(1-k)^m\), so choosing \(q\) implies \[ (1-k)^m=q \quad\Rightarrow\quad k = 1 - q^{1/m} = \Pr(H_{1,i}). \]

Prior knowledge guides \(q\): when few (if any) non-null effects are expected, \(q\) should be large; when effects are well established, \(q\) can be smaller. For small \(m\) and sufficiently small \(q\), however, the calibration can become anti-conservative: \(\Pr(H_{0,i})=1-k=q^{1/m}\) may fall below \(1/2\), which implies \(\Pr(H_{1,i})>1/2\) and therefore increases rather than decreases the odds for \(H_{1,i}\). Because the goal of the adjustment is to guard against inflated false positives, it should never move posterior probabilities in this direction; accordingly, a simple safeguard is to leave posterior probabilities unchanged whenever \(\Pr(H_{0,i})<1/2\) (equivalently, whenever \(q^{1/m}<1/2\)).

Once \(q\) is fixed, the prior odds assigned to each individual hypothesis become a deterministic function of the family size \(m\): larger families imply smaller \(k\), and consequently a higher prior probability on the null for each test.

To illustrate the impact of \(q\) on both FWER and power, we consider two data-generating regimes: a global-null scenario in which \(\theta_{1}=\cdots=\theta_{m}=0\), and an alternative scenario in which all but one effect are null (\(\theta_{1}=\cdots=\theta_{m-1}=0 \text{, } \theta_{m}=0.3\)). We then repeat the previous simulation procedure with \(\tau=2\) and \(n=50\), i.e. a setting in which pd and p-value behavior is broadly comparable, while varying only the number of tests \(m\in\{1,2,3,4,5,10,20\}\) and the prior parameter \(q\in\{0.50,0.40,0.30,0.20,0.10\}\).

While Bonferroni-adjusted p-values strictly control the FWER at the nominal level (Figure 6, Panel A), this rigor comes at the cost of substantially diminished power (Figure 6, Panel B). In contrast, the adjusted pd procedure offers a flexible trade-off governed by the prior parameter \(q\). For lower values (\(q \in \{0.10, 0.20, 0.30\}\)), the exchange is favorable: the resulting gain in power outpaces the marginal increase in FWER (Figure 6, Panel C). Notably, setting \(q=0.4\) produces FWER and power trajectories that mirror the Bonferroni adjustment. This empirical alignment is consistent with @Westfall1997 asymptotic proof that a global null probability of \(q = e^{-1} \approx 0.368\) reproduces Bonferroni-like behavior. Thus, selecting \(q \approx 0.4\) effectively calibrates the Bayesian prior to match the severity of the frequentist \(\alpha\) correction.

Accounting for correlation

A limitation of the proposed procedure is that it allocates the global-null probability across \(m\) hypotheses under an independence assumption. When hypotheses are correlated, for example, because parameters are estimated jointly in a single model or because measures share the same observations, independence-based multiplicity adjustments can be overly conservative [@Westfall1993; @Pesarin2010].

A pragmatic solution is to replace the nominal number of hypotheses \(m\) with an effective number \(m_{\text{eff}}\) that reflects the actual amount of unique information [@Cheverud2001; @Li2005]. This approach requires estimating a correlation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\) that captures the dependencies (for example among posterior distributions), then mapping \(\mathbf{R}\) to \(m_{\text{eff}}\) using an eigenvalue-based method. Multiple methods exist for estimating \(m_{\text{eff}}\), each with different assumptions. Here we apply the eigenvalue-based approach of @Cheverud2001 (see Appendix X for computational details and @Giudice2021 for critiques).

To evaluate whether this adjustment reduces conservativeness under dependence while maintaining adequate familywise error rate (FWER) control, we extended our simulation study to correlated data. We conducted a Monte Carlo simulation generating families of size \(m \in \{2,4,6,10,20\}\) with \(n = 50\) observations per dataset across \(10{,}000\) replications per condition.

In each replication, true parameter values were drawn from a multivariate normal distribution with equicorrelation structure: \(\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)\), where \(\mu_1=\cdots=\mu_{m/2}=0\) (null hypotheses) and \(\mu_{m/2+1}=\cdots=\mu_m=0.3\) (non-null hypotheses); a possible scenario were half of the null hypothesis are true. The correlation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\) had constant pairwise correlation \(\rho \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}\).

For each dataset, we obtained posterior distributions using a multivariate normal conjugate model with \(\mu = 0\) and \(\tau = 2\), and for the adjustment we set the prior parameter \(q=0.4\) based on previous results. We computed FWER under the null subset and average power [@Benjamini1995; @Grant2025], the proportion of true discoveries under \(\mu = 0.3\). We compared the multiplicity adjustment procedure when accounting for correlations (using \(m_{\text{eff}}\)) versus ignoring them (using \(m\)).

As shown in Figure 7, the method accounting for correlation provided better performance, particularly under high correlation scenarios, by maintaining proper FWER control while preserving greater power. Without this adjustment, the procedure became excessively conservative as correlation increased.

Discussion

Although pd is a descriptive posterior index, thresholding it into a decision rule and applying that rule repeatedly across many parameters induces a multiplicity problem, as the probability of at least one false claim increases with the number of decisions. The multiplicity adjustment proposed here targets this decision-level error while allowing researcher-specific knowledge to enter transparently through the prior-odds specification. We do not claim novelty; rather, we aim to increase awareness of the consequences of ignoring multiplicity. This concern is particularly acute when researchers use non-informative or weakly informative priors, or when sufficient data make the likelihood dominant.

A key practical advantage of this procedure is its applicability to pd values from different modeling strategies. While the most coherent approach analyzes all relevant data within a single joint model that explicitly represents dependence [@Gelman2012; @GelmanBook; @Kruschke2011], this is not always feasible. Researchers often report pd values from multiple separate analyses within the same manuscript [REFs]. The procedure accommodates both settings. When a joint model is available, its posterior correlation structure can be leveraged. When only separate analyses are available, the adjustment proceeds under an independence assumption. When partial correlation information is available, one can specify a correlation matrix with known values where dependence exists and zeros elsewhere.

Additionally, although we assume \(\theta_{null}=0\) throughout for simplicity, the presented procedure can be generalized to non-zero null values [@Sesoi2018]. The generalization is achieved by defining directional hypotheses relative to any chosen value, provided that within-model priors for the tested parameters are set symmetrically around this value.

An important caveat is that this framework does not provide evidence for a point null hypothesis (i.e., posterior support for \(\theta_{null}\)). Instead, it evaluates directional claims based on inequalities (e.g., \(\theta>\theta_{null}\) vs. \(\theta\le\theta_{null}\)). By construction, the complementary hypothesis includes the null value, but the framework does not assign it special status. When the null value falls outside the credible interval, one can infer its relative implausibility as a candidate value. However, this differs fundamentally from quantifying evidence in favor of the null. This contrasts with Bayes factor approaches that treat the point null as a distinct model by assigning it positive prior mass, thereby enabling direct statements of evidence supporting the null hypothesis [@Heck2023; @Morey2011; @Ly2016; add dienes]. We acknowledge that various methods exist for Bayesian hypothesis testing, each with distinct advantages and limitations.

Lastly, we argue that even when the focus is on estimation rather than hypothesis testing, multiplicity considerations remain relevant. Reported results inevitably influence future studies, both our own and others’. This influence constitutes an implicit form of decision-making. Our argument is not that researchers must always frame their work as explicit hypothesis testing, but that they should recognize how multiple comparisons affect the reliability of reported patterns. Transparency about the implied prior probabilities assigned to hypotheses helps readers evaluate the strength of claims.

For applied reporting, we recommend reporting the unadjusted pd for each parameter, along with at least one multiplicity-adjusted measure based on the proposed prior-odds correction. If no adjustment is reported, state the implied prior probability for the global null event. Notably, a similar approach to posterior odds adjustments has been implemented in JASP, a widely used software for Bayesian analysis, for post hoc testing [@Bergh2020; @deJong2019].

Despite its limitations, our method provides a coherent, practical way to recalibrate pd-based decisions for multiple comparisons, making reported claims less likely to reflect chance patterns across many parameters.

Appendix X: Eigenvalue-based approach

To account for dependence among hypotheses, we follow the eigenvalue-based approach of @Cheverud2001. This method adjusts the nominal number of tests \(m\) to an effective number \(m_{\text{eff}}\) that reflects the degree of correlation among test statistics.

Estimation procedure

  1. From the posterior covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), we derive the correlation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\).

  2. We compute the eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_m\) of \(\mathbf{R}\).

  3. We compute the observed variance of the eigenvalues as: \[ V\lambda_{\text{obs}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{m}(\lambda_i-1)^2}{m-1} \]

  4. The effective number of independent tests is: \[ m_{\text{eff}} = m\left(1-\frac{(m-1)V\lambda_{\text{obs}}}{m^2}\right) \]

The resulting \(m_{\text{eff}}\) ranges between 1 (perfect correlation among all tests) and \(m\) (complete independence), with intermediate values reflecting partial dependence.
